Dissertation research

In my dissertation, I focus on the role of digital media for contentious politics in authoritarian states. In autocracies, the internet and digital media provide a toolset for two main types of political actors: opposition and state/pro-state. While opposition actors use digital media to communicate with public, organize social movements, and challenge government monopoly over the information flow, pro-state actors, on the other hand, use media for digital repression and dissemination of propaganda and disinformation to manipulate public opinion. How— i.e., via which mechanisms and with which tools — do different political actors establish information control on digital media? What strategies are effective for reaching wide audiences? I develop a theory of political communication battle and argue that, because media is an important instrument for establishing political power, political actors struggle for the control over political communication that depends on two components: the control over the discourse and the ability to reach wide audiences.


Research interests

Political Communication: I study how political actors use digital media and information communication technologies with a focus on the role of media in social movements and in challenging authoritarian regimes.

Political Methodology: network analysis, text analysis, causal inference, maximum likelihood estimation, multi-level modeling, linear regressions, time series, Bayesian statistics.

Additional Research Interests: contentious politics, decentralization policies, local development and politics.
